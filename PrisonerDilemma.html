What is the "Prisoner's Dilemma"?


The prisoners' dilemma is a very popular example of a two-person game of strategic interaction, and it's a common introductory example in game theory. Here are the rules:

  • The two players in the game have been accused of a crime.
  • Each player is asked independently whether he is going to confess to the crime or remain silent.
  • Because each of the two players has two possible options/strategies, there are four possible outcomes to the game.
  • If both players confess, they each get sent to jail, but for fewer years than if one of the players got ratted out by the other.
  • If one player confesses and the other remains silent, the silent player gets punished severely while the player who confessed gets to go free.
  • If both players remain silent, they each get a punishment that is less severe than if they both confess.
  • In the game itself, punishments (and rewards, where relevant) are represented by utility numbers. Positive numbers represent good outcomes, negative numbers represent bad outcomes, and one outcome is better than another if the number associated with it is greater.
  • Note: In the traditional version of the game, one player can't see the move of the other player. But for our example we will assume that the players can see each other's moves.

  • If player 1 goes first and confesses, then it is in player 2's best interests to confess. They both get a lesser sentence this way.
  • If player 1 goes first and is silent, then player 2 remains silent as well in order to get the best possible reward.
  • Because each of the two players has two possible options (strategies), there are four possible outcomes to the game.
  • Similarly, if player 2 goes first and confesses, player 1 has to confess. Oterwise if he remains silent he recieves a harsher sentence.
  • If player 2 is silent, player 1 should be silent in order to get the least stringent senetnce.
  • In our version of the game, one player can see other player's moves and plan accordingly. Typically, both players are locked in seperate rooms and cannot see each other's moves.
  • In such a case, when we don't know what the other player decides, we have to confess. So that regardless of the pther player's move we get a lesser sentence.
  • There is no dominant strategy for this game.
